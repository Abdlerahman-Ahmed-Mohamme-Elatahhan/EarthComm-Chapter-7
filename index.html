Electricity and Your Community

Core Idea

Electricity is generated from many energy resources — fossil fuels, nuclear, hydro, geothermal, solar, and wind. Each has advantages and disadvantages. Fossil fuels supply ~70% of US electricity.

⚡ Electric Power vs. Electric Energy

  • Electric Power: the rate at which electricity does work at a point in time. Unit = Watt (W).
  • Electric Energy: the total amount of work done by electricity. Unit = Watt-hour (Wh).
  • Example: A 1200-W hair dryer used for 15 min uses 300 Wh of electric energy.
  • Capacity = the rate at which an appliance transforms electric energy (found on the label).
⚠️
Exam Watch

Know the difference: Power (watts, rate, moment in time) vs. Energy (watt-hours, total amount used). Expect calculation questions like the hair dryer example. Pw=ET-1 (power = energy / time)

🔥 Fossil Fuels & Steam Turbines

  • Fossil fuels (coal, petroleum, natural gas) supply ~70% of US electricity.
  • In a fossil-fueled steam turbine: fuel burned → steam produced → steam turns turbine blades → shaft turns generator → electricity produced.
  • In a nuclear steam turbine: uranium fission produces heat (instead of burning fuel) → same steam process follows.
Burn Fuel / Fission→ Steam→ Turbine→ Generator→ Electricity

💧 Hydroelectric Power

  • Water is the top renewable resource used to generate electricity.
  • 70% of US hydroelectric power comes from Pacific & Rocky Mountain states.
  • Two types:
    1. Falling-water system: dams build reservoirs → water falls through pipes → pressure turns turbine.
    2. Run-of-the-river system: river current pushes turbine blades (no water storage; depends on seasonal flow).
Advantages
  • Renewable resource
  • Cheap power source
  • Little air pollution (no combustion)
  • Limited thermal pollution vs. nuclear
  • Fast startup, adjustable output
Disadvantages
  • Dams harm local ecosystems (habitats)
  • Best sites already developed
  • Depends on seasonal water flow
  • Vulnerable to droughts

🌋 Geothermal & Other Sources

  • Renewable sources (solar, wind, biomass) + geothermal supply less than 4% of US electricity from utilities.
  • Geothermal energy: heat from inside Earth. Classified as nonrenewable but essentially inexhaustible. Most plants are in the western US (magma near surface).
  • Biomass: wood, municipal solid waste, agricultural waste — also used to generate power.
Nuclear Fission
Atomic nucleus splits → releases neutrons + vast energy (used in reactors).
Nuclear Fusion
Lightweight nuclei combine → heavier nuclei + energy (powers the Sun; not yet practical).
Renewable Resource
Can be replaced in nature at a rate close to its rate of use (e.g., water, wind, solar).
Nonrenewable Resource
Exists in a fixed amount OR used faster than it can be replaced (e.g., fossil fuels).
Photovoltaic Energy
Direct conversion of solar radiation to electricity (solar cells).
Turbine
Rotating machine that converts kinetic energy of fluid flow into mechanical rotation.

Energy From Coal

Core Idea

Coal is ancient solar energy stored as chemical energy in rock. It forms from plant remains buried over millions of years and exists in several ranks (types), each with different carbon content and heat value.

☀️ The Origin of Coal's Energy (Photosynthesis → Fossil Fuel)

  • Only 4 main energy sources exist on Earth: solar radiation, Earth's interior heat, radioactive decay, and tides.
  • Coal's energy originally comes from solar radiation, stored as chemical energy through photosynthesis.
  • Only 0.06% of solar energy reaching Earth is stored in plants — but that amount is enormous.
Photosynthesis
6CO₂ + 6H₂O + light energy → C₆H₁₂O₆ (glucose) + 6O₂
Oxidation (Respiration/Combustion)
6O₂ + C₆H₁₂O₆ → 6H₂O + 6CO₂ + energy (heat)
  • Photosynthesis and oxidation are reverse reactions of each other.
  • Most carbon returns to the atmosphere as CO₂. A very small fraction is buried in sediments → becomes peat, coal, petroleum (fossil fuels).
  • Fossil fuels are consumed far faster than they form → classified as nonrenewable.

🪨 Formation of Coal (Peat → Anthracite)

  • Coal forms from peat — unconsolidated plant remains from bogs/swamps (stems, leaves, bark visible).
  • Coal swamps: low-lying coastal freshwater areas, separated from mud/sand sources.
  • Plant material must be buried by sediment; compaction squeezes out water; methane gas escapes; carbon percentage increases.
  • As carbon % increases, coal "increases in rank".
  • It takes 35 meters of original peat to produce 1 foot of bituminous coal.
Peat→ Lignite→ Sub-bituminous→ Bituminous→ Anthracite→ Graphite

📊 Types of Coal — Carbon & Heat Content

Coal Rank Carbon Content (%) Heat Content (Btu/lb) Main Use
Lignite (brown coal) 25–35% 4,000–8,300 Electric power generation
Sub-bituminous 35–45% 8,300–11,500 Heating (low sulfur)
Bituminous 45–86% 10,500–14,000 Electricity + steel coke
Anthracite 86–98% >14,000 Home heating (scarce in eastern US)
  • Higher rank = more carbon = more heat content per pound.
  • Btu (British thermal unit) = heat needed to raise 1 lb of water by 1°F.
  • Sulfur in coal: released as SO₂ when burned → reacts with water → sulfuric acid → acid rain. Sulfur content: 1–5% depending on original plant material.
  • Ash: the part of coal that doesn't burn (sand, silt, clay). Reduces heat content and must be discarded.

⛏️ Coal Mining & Production

  • In 2009, 45% of US electricity was produced from coal.
  • Coal holds 92% of all fossil fuel reserves — the largest reserve of any fossil fuel.
  • Underground mining methods: drift, slope, shaft mining.
  • Surface mining methods: area, contour, mountaintop removal, auger mining.
  • Three factors determining if coal is economical to mine:
    1. Transportation cost to where it is used.
    2. Environmental concerns of mining and use.
    3. Quality, thickness, volume, and depth of the coal.

Energy From Petroleum and Natural Gas

Core Idea

Petroleum (crude oil) and natural gas originate from buried organic matter in sedimentary rocks. They migrate through porous rocks until trapped by seals, forming reservoirs. The US is heavily dependent on oil but is running low on domestic reserves.

🛢️ Nature & Origin of Petroleum and Natural Gas

  • Petroleum (crude oil): liquid made mainly of organic compounds (simple to complex).
  • Natural gas: mainly methane (CH₄), a single organic compound.
  • Raw material: remains of tiny marine plants and animals (plankton) deposited with sediments on ocean/lake floors.
  • As sediments are buried deeper → temperature and pressure increase → organic matter undergoes maturation → converted into oil and gas.
  • Petroleum geologists call the right burial depth the "oil window" or "gas window", and the source region "the kitchen."
  • Oil and gas are less dense than water → they rise through porous rocks.

🧱 Porosity, Permeability, Seals & Reservoirs

  • Porosity: % of pore space in a rock = (volume of pores ÷ bulk volume) × 100. Loose sand = 30–40% porosity.
  • Permeability: how easily fluids flow through connected pores in response to pressure differences. High porosity alone is not enough — pores must be connected.
  • Cement: new mineral material deposited around sand grains during burial → reduces porosity.
  • Source rocks: mudstone/shale rich in organic matter where oil and gas are generated (impermeable → fluids move slowly).
  • Seal: impermeable layer with convex-upward shape (like an umbrella) that traps oil and gas from rising to the surface.
  • Reservoir: large body of porous + permeable sedimentary rock containing trapped oil/gas below a seal. Good reservoir rocks = high porosity + high permeability.
  • Closure: vertical distance from the top of the seal to the spill point — giant reservoirs can have closures of tens of meters and hold hundreds of millions of barrels.

🔧 Recovery of Petroleum

  • Oil removed by drilling deep wells (vertical, then diverted sideways at precise angles).
  • Sometimes oil flows under its own pressure; usually it must be pumped out.
  • Much oil remains trapped in pore spaces even after years of production (adheres to pore walls).
  • Secondary recovery: techniques to recover remaining oil — most common is injecting hot steam into the well to make oil flow more easily.

📈 History & Production in the US

  • First successful oil drilling: Colonel Edwin Drake, 1859, Pennsylvania (70 ft depth).
  • Spindletop Oil Field, Texas, 1901 — massive discovery that boosted petroleum industry.
  • By 1947, use of petroleum + natural gas exceeded coal.
  • In 1920, US produced >2/3 of world's oil. By 2009, only 11% of world's oil.
  • US has less than 35% of its original oil remaining.
  • Texas = largest oil-producing state since late 1920s.
  • Sedimentary basins = areas where sediments accumulate to great thicknesses; where oil/gas are found.

🔮 Future of Petroleum & Alternatives

  • USGS estimates: 95% probability = at least 2,250 billion barrels globally; 50% estimate = ~3,000 billion barrels.
  • Future production will level off then decline → growing shortage → need for alternative energy.
  • Tar sands (sandstones with solid organic matter) and oil shales already producing and will be major future sources. Canada is especially rich in tar sands.
  • Major exploration frontier = deep offshore ocean areas.
  • Arctic National Wildlife Refuge (ANWR) — believed to contain billions of barrels; drilling highly debated.
Petroleum (Crude Oil)
Oily, flammable liquid of organic compounds produced in sedimentary rocks during burial of organic matter.
Natural Gas
Gas (mainly methane CH₄) produced in sedimentary rocks during burial of organic matter.
Feedstock
Raw material (e.g., petroleum) supplied to a processing plant to produce manufactured material (e.g., plastics).
Source Rocks
Sedimentary rocks rich in organic matter where petroleum and natural gas are generated.
Secondary Recovery
Techniques (like injecting hot steam) to recover oil still trapped in sediments after primary production.
Sedimentary Basin
Area of Earth's crust where sediments accumulate to great thicknesses — where oil/gas are found.

Environmental Impacts and Energy Consumption

Core Idea

Burning fossil fuels produces CO₂ (greenhouse effect, global warming) and sulfur dioxide (acid rain). Oil spills devastate marine and coastal ecosystems. Both problems can be partially mitigated by technology, but require understanding of chemistry and Earth systems.

🌍 Fossil Fuels and the Carbon Cycle

  • Fossil fuels supply 85% of primary energy in the US and produce 98% of US CO₂ emissions.
  • CO₂ produced per unit of energy: natural gas emits ~½ as much as coal; petroleum ~¾ as much as coal.
  • First Law of Thermodynamics: energy cannot be created or destroyed — only converted (chemical energy → heat energy when fuel burned).
  • Law of Conservation of Matter: matter cannot be created or destroyed. Carbon from fossil fuels never disappears — it moves between Earth system reservoirs.
  • CO₂ has increased by ~25% in the last 150 years due to burning fossil fuels + deforestation.
  • Net anthropogenic CO₂ emissions: ~6.6 billion metric tons/year; ~3.3 billion metric tons added to atmosphere annually.
  • Greenhouse gases (CO₂, water vapor, methane): slow the escape of heat from Earth to space → contribute to global warming.

🌧️ Coal and Acid Rain

  • pH scale (0–14): pH <7 = acidic; pH 7 = neutral; pH >7 = basic/alkaline. Each whole number = 10× change in concentration.
  • Pure water = pH 7.0 (neutral). Natural rainwater = pH ~5.7 (mildly acidic — CO₂ dissolves to form carbonic acid).
  • Acid rain is caused by: coal contains up to 5% sulfur → burned → SO₂ gas released → reacts with O₂ then H₂O → sulfuric acid (H₂SO₄).
Acid Rain Formation
SO₃ + H₂O → H₂SO₄ (sulfuric acid)
H₂SO₄ → H⁺ + HSO₃⁻ → 2H⁺ + SO₄²⁻
  • Burning fuels also produces nitrogen oxides → nitric acid — another acid rain contributor.
  • Acid rain most severe in the Northeast US because: coal power plants are in the Midwest (Ohio, Michigan, Illinois); prevailing winds blow west-to-east; bedrock in the Northeast is granite (cannot neutralize acid).
  • Where bedrock is limestone or marble: rock dissolves and neutralizes acid → buffers the pH.
  • Acid rain effects: kills fish, insects, aquatic plants, plankton → "dead lakes" (entire food web disrupted).

🏭 Scrubbing Technology

  • Process: flue-gas desulfurization (scrubbing) removes SO₂ from power plant exhaust.
  • Wet scrubber: flue gas sprayed with calcium carbonate slurry → SO₂ → calcium sulfate (CaSO₄ / gypsum). Can remove up to 95% of SO₂.
  • Useful by-product: pure gypsum → used to make plaster and wallboard.

🌊 Oil Spills and the Environment

  • Global oil consumption grew from 21 million barrels/day (1960) to over 84 million barrels/day (2009).
  • Oil spills occur during: transfer to vessels, ocean transport, pipeline breaks, drilling operations.
  • Environmental impacts:
    • Waves and currents move oil on shore → oil saturates beach sand/gravel → harms coastal vegetation.
    • Rocks/boulders coated with oil → toxic to coastal wildlife.
    • Marine life poisoned and smothered (oil is sticky + toxic).
    • Disrupts marine food chain; bottom organic matter contaminated.
    • Human health: inhaling fumes, eating contaminated fish/shellfish.
    • Economic: housing values drop, recreational activities decline.

⚓ Deepwater Horizon (Case Study — KNOW THIS!)

  • Date: April 20, 2010 — explosion on offshore platform in Gulf of Mexico, ~80 km SE of Mississippi River delta.
  • Platform sank in ~1,500 m of water 2 days later. 11 workers never found.
  • Blowout preventer failed. 125-ton containment dome failed.
  • Oil flow stopped: July 15, 2010 — 86 days after the explosion. Then cement sealed the well.
  • Leaked oil: approximately 4.9 million barrels.
  • Oil slick: up to 75,000 km² (≈ size of South Carolina).
  • Oil reached shores of Louisiana, Mississippi, Alabama, Florida.
  • Cleanup methods: fire booms (U-shaped towed devices for controlled burning), dispersants (chemicals), sand berms.
  • Considered the worst environmental disaster in US history.
⚠️
Exam Watch

Be ready to recall specific Deepwater Horizon facts: date, location, duration, amount of oil, area of slick, cleanup methods, and its status as the worst US environmental disaster.

Carbon Cycle
Continuous exchange and recycling of carbon through the Earth system (atmosphere, biosphere, geosphere, hydrosphere).
Greenhouse Gases
Gases (CO₂, water vapor, methane) that absorb long-wavelength radiation emitted by Earth, slowing heat loss to space.
Acid (pH <7)
Solution with high concentration of hydrogen ions (H⁺). Each pH unit = 10× more acidic than next higher value.
Scrubbing
Flue-gas desulfurization — removes SO₂ and other pollutants from combustion gases before release.
Anthropogenic
Generated or produced by human activities (e.g., anthropogenic CO₂ emissions from burning fossil fuels).

Renewable Energy Sources — Solar and Wind

Core Idea

Solar energy comes from nuclear fusion in the Sun and reaches Earth as electromagnetic radiation. It can be used for heating (active/passive) and electricity generation (photovoltaics). Wind power is also solar-derived and increasingly important for electricity generation.

☀️ How Solar Energy Works

  • Solar energy is produced in the Sun's core through nuclear fusion (hydrogen atoms fused → helium atoms; tiny amount of mass → energy).
  • Sun's surface radiates electromagnetic energy in all directions — mostly visible light, plus ultraviolet (shorter) and infrared (longer) wavelengths.
  • Solar radiation travels at the speed of light; reaches Earth in about 8 minutes.
  • Only a very small fraction of solar energy reaches Earth — but more than enough for all of Earth's needs if practically captured.
  • The Sun has been producing energy for ~5 billion years and will continue for a few billion more.

📐 Insolation — Factors Affecting Solar Energy Receipt

  • Insolation: the rate at which a given area of land receives solar energy (W/m²).
  • Maximum insolation = ~1,000 W/m² (Sun directly overhead, clear sky, sea level).
  • Factors that affect insolation:
    • Latitude (higher latitude = more atmosphere to pass through)
    • Season (Earth's axis tilted 23.5° → summer = longer days, higher Sun)
    • Time of day
    • Cloudiness of the sky
    • Clearness of the air
    • Slope of the land surface (perpendicular to Sun's rays = maximum)
  • Earth's axis tilted 23.5° from the plane of its orbit around the Sun. Points toward Sun in Northern Hemisphere summer → long days, high Sun; points away in winter → short days, low Sun.

🏠 Solar Heating Systems

Active Solar Heating
  • Uses special solar collector equipment
  • Collects and distributes solar energy mechanically
  • More effective than passive
  • Solar collector = thin flat box with transparent cover and black plate
  • Cool water circulates through → warmed → stored
Passive Solar Heating
  • Home designed to let in large amounts of sunlight
  • No special mechanical equipment
  • Less effective than active
  • Large south-facing windows + overhangs
  • Materials in floors/walls absorb & store heat by day, release at night
  • Solar water heating efficiency: heating water with fossil fuels is only 60% efficient; electric water heaters ~90%; but electricity from fossil fuels is only 30–40% efficient.
  • Solar water heating also eliminates air pollution from water heating.

⚡ Photovoltaics (PV Cells / Solar Cells)

  • Photovoltaic (PV) cells convert light energy directly into electricity.
  • Used in: toys, calculators, roadside phones, water pumps, communications equipment, home appliances.
  • Efficiency is not high but is increasing as new materials are developed.
  • In many remote cases, PV power is the cheapest form of electricity available.

💨 Wind Power

  • Wind power used for water pumping for hundreds of years; electricity generation started about 35 years ago.
  • Triple-blade propeller = most popular wind turbine design.
  • Texas leads the US in wind power generation (2010: 9,727 MW capacity; tens of thousands of turbines).
  • Other high-potential areas: Rocky Mountains, flat Midwest states, Alaska.
  • Commercial wind turbine blades can be up to 60 m in diameter.
  • Can be used on large scale (wind farms) or small scale (households).
⚠️
Exam Watch

Solar energy must occupy a MUCH larger area per unit of energy than fossil fuels because insolation is spread out over a large area and is not very intense in any small area. This is a fundamental limitation of solar power — know how to explain it.

Earth's Ore Resources

Core Idea

Most ore resources are found on continents, especially in orogenic belts. Ores form through specific geological processes (especially hydrothermal activity). Finding them requires expensive, multi-technique exploration — mapping, geochemistry, and geophysics.

🌊 Ore Resources in the Ocean

  • Ocean water contains huge amounts of chemical elements — but concentrations are extremely small → too expensive to extract most of them.
  • Example: Gold in ocean water = only 0.011 µg per liter → over 10 billion kg total, but extraction is uneconomical.
  • Iron–manganese nodules: dark, rounded masses on deep ocean floor (especially Pacific). Range from golf-ball to fist size. Contain iron, manganese, and other elements. Mining techniques exist but:
    • Sediment stirred up would harm deep-ocean environments.
    • Open oceans belong to all of humanity — ownership issues.

⛰️ Mineral Resources of the Continents

  • Oldest continental bedrock = 4 billion years old (Earth itself = 4.5 billion years).
  • Most rocks (sandstone, shale, limestone, granite, basalt, schist, gneiss) do NOT contain economically valuable minerals. However, many are used as building stones.
  • Metal mining takes up less than 0.1% of total US land area.
  • Orogenic belts (mountain-building regions) = areas where lithospheric plate collisions caused uplift. These areas are most associated with ores (igneous activity → ore deposition).
  • Most ore-forming regions are in the western US (complex geology) and some eastern US areas.

🔥 Hydrothermal Activity — Most Important Ore-Forming Process

  • Magmas contain small amounts of valuable chemical elements.
  • These elements become concentrated in water-rich "juices" left over after most of magma crystallizes.
  • Juices work upward → cool → unusual minerals crystallize → hydrothermal ore deposits.
  • Metals from hydrothermal deposits: copper, zinc, tin, lead, mercury, gold, silver, platinum.

🔩 Iron and Aluminum — The Two Most-Used Metals

  • Iron ore: from special iron-rich sedimentary rocks deposited in ancient oceans. Mined in northern Minnesota and northern Michigan.
  • Aluminum ore (bauxite): aluminum oxides formed by weathering of aluminum-bearing rocks in warm, humid climates. Some mined in US, but most imported.

🔍 Finding Minerals — Exploration Techniques

  • Drilling = most expensive exploration method ($150/m for mineral holes; millions of dollars for oil holes).
  • Geologists use multiple techniques to narrow down areas before drilling.
Technique Who Uses It What It Does
Mapping Geologists Maps rock types, folds, faults, fractures, unusual colors at surface. Uses "guides to ore."
Geochemistry Geochemists Analyzes chemistry of rocks, soil, vegetation, water. Chemical "halos" indicate buried deposits.
Seismic-Wave Reflection Geophysicists Small explosion/strike vibration → reflects off rock layers → geophone records return time → reveals depth, folds, faults.
Gravity (Gravimetry) Geophysicists Gravimeter detects local variations in Earth's gravity. Ores are denser → slightly stronger gravity field above them.
Magnetometry Geophysicists Magnetometer detects changes in Earth's magnetic field. Iron-bearing rocks affect local magnetic field.
Electrical Conductivity Geophysicists Current put into ground. Metallic minerals conduct electricity well — indicates ore presence.
  • Geophone: seismic detector placed on/in the ground that records ground motion.
  • Gravimeter: instrument measuring variations in Earth's gravitational field.
  • Magnetometer: instrument measuring variations in Earth's magnetic field.
  • Millions of dollars spent on exploration — only a very small number of explored areas become profitable mines.

The Costs and Benefits of Mining Minerals

Core Idea

Minerals are extracted using surface mining (open-pit, strip, quarry) or underground mining. The method depends on ore depth, size, grade, and cost. Mining has significant environmental impacts, requiring reclamation, and poses safety risks to workers.

🏔️ Surface Mining

  • Most minerals in the US are removed by surface mining.
  • Open-pit mining: large ore body close to surface. Earth-moving equipment removes soil and overburden → series of benches and roads wind into a large deep pit → ore drilled, blasted, loaded into trucks → transported to crushers/refineries.
  • Topsoil stored in piles for later reclamation. Overburden called spoil.
  • Example: Bingham Canyon copper mine, near Salt Lake City, Utah — one of the largest open-pits in the world (~2 miles diameter, >½ mile deep).
  • Strip mining: used for thin, widespread, sheet-like deposits near the surface (much of western US coal; future oil shale in Colorado/Wyoming).
  • Dimension stone quarries: large blocks of granite, marble, limestone, or sandstone removed intact (not crushed).
  • Resources from surface mines: sand, gravel, coal, phosphate, iron ore, copper ore.

⛏️ Underground Mining

  • Used when mineral resources lie deep beneath the surface OR the ore body has irregular geometry.
  • Shafts and tunnels provide access and ventilation (vertical, horizontal, inclined, or corkscrew-shaped depending on ore orientation).
  • Ore hauled to surface by trains, loaders, trucks, or elevators.
  • Large "rooms" formed when ore is removed; pillars of ore/waste rock left to support the mine roof.
  • Modern technique: back-filling tunnels → recovers ore pillars, prevents cave-ins, prevents acid mine drainage.
  • Trackless semi-automated equipment reduces number of people exposed to underground hazards.

🌿 Environmental and Safety Concerns

  • Surface mining: removes overburden + vegetation → destroys ecosystems; changes water runoff patterns; dissolved chemicals from exposed rocks can pollute surface and groundwater.
  • All US states require reclamation plans before any ground is disturbed:
    1. Spread spoil back over mined area to restore topography.
    2. Spread available topsoil from original stripping over spoil.
    3. Replant native trees and shrubs.
  • Most mining deaths in the US = poorly maintained equipment + failure to wear safety gear + working in unsafe locations.
  • Underground collapses are much less frequent in the US than other countries.
  • Hazardous materials exposure: silica dust, heavy metals, asbestos fibers can cause serious illnesses. Exposure must be constantly monitored and limited.
Surface Mining Advantages
  • Less expensive for large, shallow deposits
  • Safer for workers (no tunnel collapse risk)
  • Can use large equipment for efficiency
  • Better ore recovery
Surface Mining Disadvantages
  • Destroys ecosystems and vegetation
  • Alters water runoff patterns
  • Chemical pollution of water
  • Large land area disturbed
Spoil
Overburden and other waste material removed in mining, quarrying, dredging, or excavating.
Overburden
Rock material that lies over a mineral deposit and must be removed prior to mining.
Open-Pit Mining
Surface mining method where large equipment removes soil/rock above a large, near-surface ore body in a series of benches.
Strip Mining
Surface mining for thin, widespread, sheet-like deposits near the surface (e.g., western US coal, future oil shale).
Ore
A rock that contains valuable minerals in concentrations worth mining economically.
